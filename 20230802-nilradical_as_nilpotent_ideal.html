nilradical in an artinian ring as nilpotent ideal

1. Proposition

Let \(A\) be an Artinian ring and \(\mathcal{N}_A\) the nilradical. Then there exists an \(n \in \mathbb{N}\) such that

\begin{align*} (\mathcal{N}_A)^n = 0 \end{align*}

2. Proof

Let

\begin{align*} \mathcal{N}_A^1 \subseteq \mathcal{N}_A^2 ... \end{align*}

Since \(R\) is artinian, the Chain stabilizes, i.e.

\begin{align*} I \coloneqq \mathcal{N}_A^k = \mathcal{N}_A^{k+1} \exists k \in \mathbb{N} \end{align*}

Suppose \(I \neq 0\) and let

\begin{align*} \sigma = \{J \subseteq R \vert J \text{ Ideal, } J \cdot I \neq 0\} \end{align*}

Then there exists an minimal \(J \in \sigma\) (see: artinian ring and minimal ideal) and therefore an \(x \in J\) such that \(x \cdot I \neq 0\) w.l.o.g. \(J = (x)\). Then we get

\begin{align*} ((x)I) \cdot I =& (x) I^2 \\ =& (x) I \\ \neq 0 \end{align*}

and therefore \((x)I \subseteq (x)\) with \((x)I \in \sigma\) Hence we get \(x = xy\) for \(y \in \mathcal{N}_A^k \subseteq \mathcal{N}_A\)

By induction, it follows that

\begin{align*} x =& xy^n \\ =& x \cdot 0 \\ =& 0 \end{align*}

Therefore \((x) = 0\), Contradiction.
