element of a finite ring as unit or zero divisor

1. Proposition

Let \(A\) be a finite ring. Then \(\alpha \in A\) is either a unit or a zero divisor

2. Proof

Suppose \(\alpha\) is not a zero divisor.

Let

\begin{align*} \alpha_r: R \rightarrow& R \\ x \mapsto& r \cdot x \end{align*}

by distributivity of a ring, it is a group-homomorphism on the underlying group.

Since \(\alpha\) is not a zero divisor, it follows, that \(\mathrm{ker}(\alpha_r) = \{0\}\). Hence \(\alpha_r\) is injective (cf. minimal kernel and monomorphism).

Thus by set-theoretic <Äquivalenz von Bijektivität, Surjektivität und Injektivität bei gleich mächtigen, endlichen Mengen>, it follows that \(\varphi\) is bijective. Hence there exists an \(x \in R\) s.t.

\begin{align*} \alpha_r(x) = r \cdot x = 1 \end{align*}
