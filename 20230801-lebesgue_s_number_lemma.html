lebesgue's number lemma

1. Proposition

Let \((M,d)\) a compact metric space and \(\mathcal{U}\) an open covering. Then there exists a lebesgue number \(\delta > 0\).

2. Proof

Let \(\mathcal{U}\) be an open covering, then there exists a finite subcover \(U_1,...,U_n\). Let w.l.o.g. \(U_1,...,U_n \neq M\), otherwise any \(\delta\) suffices. We define

\begin{align*} f(x) \coloneqq \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{1}{n} d(x, M \setminus U_i) \end{align*}

\(f\) is a continuous map (see: topological group)

Then for each \(x \in M\), since \(U_1,...,U_n\) is a covering, there exists an \(U_m\) with \(x \in U_m\). Hence \(d(x, M \setminus U_m) > 0\) (see: positiver Abstand zu einer Abgeschlosenen Menge)

Furthermore, since \(M\) is compact, \(f[M]\) attains a minimum value (see: allgemeiner Satz vom Minimum und Maximum) Let \(\delta > 0\) be smaller than \(f[M]\).

Suppose \(Y \subseteq M\) open with \(\mathrm{diam}(Y) < \delta\). w.l.o.g. let \(Y\) be nonempty with \(y \in Y\), then \(Y \subseteq B_{\delta}(y)\). Additionally, \(f(y) > \delta\), i.e. there exists an \(U_k\) such that \(d(y, M \setminus U_k) > \delta\). Therefore, we conclude, that

\begin{align*} B_{\delta}(y) \subseteq U_k \end{align*}
