noetherian space and minimal element of finite intersections of closed subsets

1. Proposition

Let \((X, \mathcal{T})\) be a topological space

  1. \(X\) is a noetherian space
  2. Let \(S_i \subseteq X\) be family of subsets and \(\mathcal{S}\) the set of all finite intersections.

Then there exists a minimal element

2. Proof

2.1. 1) \(\implies\) 2)

Proof by contraposition: Let \(S_i \subseteq \mathcal{P}(X)\) be a subset. Suppose there doesn't exist a minimal element for \(\mathcal{S}\). Then we can inductively construct a sequence $\mathcal{A}1 \supseteq \mathcal{A}2 …$ with \(\mathcal{A}_1 = S_{i(1)}\) and \(\mathcal{A}_{n+1} = \mathcal{A}_n \cap S_{i(n+1)}\)

Therefore, the chain \((\mathcal{A}_{n})\) can't stabilize, since then \(\mathcal{A}_m\) would be a minimal element

2.2. 2) \(\implies\) 1)

Let $S1 \supseteq S2 \supseteq …$ be a Chain. Then \(\mathcal{S}\) contains a minimal element \(S = \bigcap_{j = 1}^n S_{i(j)}\) and w.l.o.g. we can assume \(S = S_{\mathrm{max}(i(j))}\), since \(S_{j(i)} \supseteq S_{\mathrm{max}(i(j))}\) Therefore we conclude

\begin{align*} S_{\mathrm{max}(i(j))} \subseteq S_n \forall n \in \mathbb{N} \end{align*}

because otherwise, \(S_n \cap S \subseteq S_{\mathrm{max}(i(j))}\) and hence \(S\) wouldn't be a minimal element.
