0-Ring of a graded Ring as subring

1. Proposition

Let \(M\) be a monoid and \(R = \bigoplus_{m \in m} R_m\) a graded ring Then \(R_0 \subseteq R\) for the neutral element \(0 \in M\) is a subring

2. Proof

2.1. abelian group

follows from assumption of \(R_m\) beeing an abelian group

2.2. distributivity

is inherited from \(R\)

2.3. multiplicatively closed

for \(r,s \in R_0\), we conclude

\begin{align*} r \cdot s \in& R_0 \cdot R_0 \\ \subseteq& R_{0+0} \\ =& R_0 \end{align*}

2.4. mutliplicative neutral element

let \(r \in R\), we show, that there exists an \(r_0 = 1 \in R_0\) such that \(r_0 \cdot r = r \cdot r_0 = r\). w.l.o.g. we will show it only for any \(r_n \in R\), the general case follows from applying distributivity

By assumption there exist a a sum

\begin{align*} 1 = \sum_{m \in M} r_m \end{align*}

for \(r_m \in R_m\) such that for almost all \(m \in M: r_m = 0\)

Thus we get for any \(r_n \in R_n\)

\begin{align*} r_n =& 1 \cdot r_n \\ =& \left(\sum_{m \in M} r_m \right) \cdot r_n \\ =& \sum_{m \in M} r_m \cdot r_n \end{align*}

Since any Element is uniquely given by a sum, - \(R\) is after all the direct sum - we conclude, that \(r_n = (0,...,0,r_n,0,...)\) and hence \(r_{m} \cdot r_{k} = 0 \in R_{m+k}\) for \(k \neq 0\) and thus get

\begin{align*} r_n = r_0 \cdot r_n \end{align*}

Thus it is a left-sided inverse for any \(r \in R\), hence the neutral element uniquely defined by left/right neutral element

hence \(r_0 = 1\)
