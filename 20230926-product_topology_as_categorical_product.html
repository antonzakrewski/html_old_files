product topology as categorical product

1. Proposition

Let \((X_i, \mathcal{T}_i)\) be a family of topological spaces. Then the product topology is the categorical product in the category Top

2. Proof

2.1. product topology as categorical product

Let

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} Y \arrow[]{d}{\varphi} & \arrow[]{rd}{\pi_i \circ \varphi} \prod_{i \in I} X_i \arrow[]{r}{\pi_i}& X_i \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

By the statement about the product topology as coarsest topology for a continuous projection, it follows, that \(\pi_i\) is continuous, hence a morphism. Furthermore, let

\begin{align*} \varphi: Y \rightarrow& \prod_{i \in I} X_i \\ Y \mapsto (\pi_i \circ \varphi(y))_i \end{align*}

By universal property, since every \(\pi_i \circ \varphi\) is continuous, it follows, that \(\varphi\) is continuous, hence again a morphism

2.2. categorical product as product topology

  1. \(\pi_i \circ \varphi\) is continuous \(\implies \varphi\) is continuous

By assumption, there exists a unique morphism, making this diagram commute

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} Y \arrow[]{rd}{\pi_i \circ \varphi} \arrow[swap]{d}{\exists! \psi}& \\ \prod X_i \arrow[swap]{r}{\pi_i} & X_i \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

By uniqueness, we conclude, that the set theoretic map is \(\varphi = \psi\) and since it is a morphism, \(\varphi\) is continuous

  1. \(\varphi\) is continuous is continuous \(\implies\) \(\pi_i \circ \varphi\)

follows from composition of continuous maps
