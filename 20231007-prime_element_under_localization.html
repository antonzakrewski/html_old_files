prime element under localization

1. Proposition

In general not right ?

Let \(A\) be a commutative ring, \(S \subseteq A\) a multiplicatively closed subset and \(\beta \in A \setminus S\) prime 1

TFAE:

  1. for any prime \(\alpha \in A \setminus S\) it follows that \(\frac{\alpha}{1} \in S^{-1}A\) is also prime
  2. \(0 \not\in S\)

2. Proof

2.1. 1) \(\implies\) 2)

Suppose \(0 \in S\), then \(S^{-1}A = 0\) (cf. localization as zero ring) Thus \(\frac{\beta}{1} = 0\) and hence by definition, \(\beta\) is not prime

2.2. 2) \(\implies\) 1)

Suppose \(\alpha \in A \setminus S\) is prime, then for \(\frac{r_1}{s_1}, \frac{r_2}{s_2} \in S^{-1}A\) with

\begin{align*} \frac{\alpha}{1} \mid \frac{r_1}{s_1} \cdot \frac{r_2}{s_1} \end{align*}

it follows that there exists an \(\frac{r_3}{s_3} \in S^{-1}A\) such that

\begin{align*} \alpha \cdot \frac{r_3}{s_3} =& \frac{r_1}{s_1} \cdot \frac{r_2}{s_2} \\ \alpha \cdot r_3 \cdot s_1 \cdot s_2 =& s_3 \cdot r_1 \cdot r_2 \end{align*}

Hence \(\alpha \mid s_3 \cdot (r_1 \cdot r_2)\) in \(R\)
