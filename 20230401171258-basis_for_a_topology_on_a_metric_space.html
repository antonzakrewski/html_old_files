Basis for a topology on a metric space

1. Proposition

Let \((M,d)\) be a metric space and \((M,\mathcal{T})\) be the topology induced by the metric \(d\). Then the collection \(\mathcal{B} := \{B_{\varepsilon}(m) \vert m \in M, \varepsilon > 0\}\) of epsilon-neighbourhoods \(B_{\varepsilon}(m)\) is a basis for the natural topology.

2. Proof

2.1. basis

\(\bigcup_{B \in \mathcal{B}} B = M\) is trivial - e.g. choose for \(m \in M : B_{1}(m)\) For \(B_{\varepsilon}(m) \cap B_{\varepsilon'}(m')\) we can construct a suitable union. For \(b \in C := B_{\varepsilon}(m) \cap B_{\varepsilon'}(m')\) we can embed an epsilon-neighbourhood using the triangle inequality:

\begin{align*} \forall b \in B_{\varepsilon}(m) : d(m,c) \leq d(m,b) + d(b,c) \Rightarrow B_{\varepsilon - d(m,b)}(b) \subseteq B_{\varepsilon}(m) \end{align*}

with \(\varepsilon - d(m,b) > 0\) because of \(d(m,b) < \varepsilon\) For \(c \in C\) we can take \(\mathrm{min}(\varepsilon - d(m,b), \varepsilon' - d(m',b))\) and unite those sets

2.2. equal topology

by definition, every union of sets from \(\mathcal{B}\) is open (metric space). Suppose \(O\) open, then for each \(o \in O\) we can choose an \(\varepsilon > 0\), such that \(B_{\varepsilon}(o) \subseteq O\). Therefore the union of those \(B_{\varepsilon}(o)\) is \(O\) itself
