monomorphism in general not injective

1. Proposition

Let \(\mathcal{C}\) be a concrete category and \(f \in \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(A, B)\) a monomorphism. Then \(f\) is in general not injective

2. Proof

Let \(\mathrm{divAb}\) be the category of divisible groups.

\begin{align*} \pi: \mathbb{Q} \rightarrow& \mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z} \\ \frac{p}{q} \mapsto& \frac{p}{q} + \mathbb{Z} \end{align*}

Let \((G,+)\) be a divisible group (possibly nonabelian) and \(f,g: G \rightarrow \mathbb{Q}\) morphisms such that

\begin{align*} \pi \circ f = \pi \circ g \end{align*}

Suppose there exists an \(x \in G\) such that

\begin{align*} (\pi \circ f)(x) = (\pi \circ g)(x) \end{align*}

but \(f(x) \neq g(x)\) Thus we conclude, that \(f(x) - g(x) = n \neq 0\), w.l.o.g. \(n > 0\). Since \(G\) is a divisible group, there exists an \(y \in G\) such that \(y^{2n} = x\). Thus

\begin{align*} f(x) =& f(2n \cdot y) \\ =& 2n \cdot f(y) \\ \Rightarrow f(y) =& \frac{1}{2n} f(x) \end{align*}

and analoguosly for \(g\) Furthermore we get

\begin{align*} f(y) - g(y) =& \frac{1}{2n} (f(x) - g(x)) \\ =& \frac{1}{2n} n \\ =& \frac{1}{2} \end{align*}

but since \(\pi\) is a monomorphism, we conclude that

\begin{align*} \pi \circ f(y) =& \pi \circ g(y) \\ \Rightarrow f(y) - g(y) \in \mathbb{Z} \end{align*}

Hence we have a Contradiction
