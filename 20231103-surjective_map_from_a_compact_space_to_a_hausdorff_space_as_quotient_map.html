surjective map from a compact space to a hausdorff space as quotient map

1. Proposition

Let \((X,\mathcal{T})\) and \((Y,\mathcal{T}_Y)\) be topological spaces and \(f: X \rightarrow Y\) a surjective, continuous map. Suppose \(X\) is compact and \(Y\) is Hausdorff, then \(f\) is a quotient map.

2. Proof

2.1. a) \(B \subseteq Y\) open \(\implies\) \(f^{-1}[B]\) open

follows from continuouity of \(f\)

2.2. b) \(f^{-1}[B]\) open \(\implies\) \(B\) open

Suppose \(f^{-1}[B]\) is open. Then \(X \ f^{-1}[B]\) is closed. Thus \(X \setminus f^{-1}[B]\) is compact (cf. closed subset of a compact space as compact set) and by image of compact space as compact space, it follows that

\begin{align*} f[X \setminus f^{-1}[B]] \end{align*}

is compact and by Hausdorffness of \(Y\) also closed (cf. closedness of compact sets in Hausdorff spaces) Furthermore, since \(f\) is surjective, it follows, that begin{align*} f[X ∖ f-1[B]] = Y ∖ B \end{align*} (see: image of a preimage and complement, surjective image of preimage and complement)
